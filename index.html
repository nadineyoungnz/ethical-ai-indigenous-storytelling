Primary Research Question

The
Kaupapa

How can AI technologies be governed and applied in ways that protect and enhance Māori language, storytelling, and cultural integrity in Aotearoa, while upholding Indigenous data sovereignty, tikanga-based governance, and whānau/hapū/iwi control — and what lessons can be drawn from comparative Indigenous examples?

Sub Q 1

What are the ethical risks and opportunities of using AI for Māori language and cultural revitalisation?

Sub Q 2

How do Indigenous-led AI initiatives differ from commercial approaches in governance and community benefit?

Sub Q 3

What ethical frameworks and governance principles guide AI use in Indigenous language contexts?

Sub Q 4

How do Indigenous communities define cultural integrity in AI design and use for storytelling?

Findings

Five
Interconnected
Themes

These themes emerged through thematic analysis of ten interviews and twenty survey responses. They are deeply interconnected and form a coherent narrative about where AI currently sits in relation to Māori language, knowledge, and data.

01

AI as a Crossroads of Risk and Opportunity

AI is not being experienced as neutral. It is understood as a critical point of decision — a moment where choices made now will shape future outcomes. Disengagement is not a safe option; choosing not to engage does not remove AI's influence, it shifts control elsewhere. A double digital divide emerged: not only lack of access, but lack of AI literacy.

"We're at a crossroads. We can either say, no, AI is bad, let's ignore it, and then we will be recolonised, but in a way that no one can comprehend right now… the risk is if we don't actually grasp it now."

— Karaitiana Taiuru, interview 2025

"There are so many people whose digital literacy was already low before AI. What AI does is take that gap and turn it into a chasm."

— Smith, interview 2025

"I think there is a risk that the tools don't work. That's the main risk. I've seen too much hype around AI and people getting disappointed."

— Research participant, interview 2025
02

Data Sovereignty as a Non-Negotiable Foundation

Before considering how AI might support Māori language or cultural revitalisation, the question of data must be addressed first. Every participant returned to data sovereignty as the starting point. The tapu/noa/rāhui classification framework provides a practical mechanism for translating sovereignty principles into applied governance decisions.

"We get brought in at the eighth hour and it's like, well, everything's already happened. It's a rubber stamp. The opportunity to influence and co-design has been and gone."

— Research participant, interview 2025

"We had to work out how to identify someone's whakapapa early on in the conversation to determine which buckets to put the data in — and then who had authority to let iwi data be used for another purpose, and how would we get that authority if we needed to?"

— Research participant, interview 2025
"We are a bicultural country… maybe there's a bit of, I don't want to get something wrong. Whereas if we did a little bit more work about what that means, we could actually move faster."— Young, C., interview, 2025
03

Governance Must Be Tikanga-Based, Not Tokenistic

Having Māori present in a process is not the same as having Māori governance. Tokenistic inclusion does not simply fall short — it can actively cause harm by creating the appearance of cultural safety without any real shift in decision-making power. Effective governance requires collective expertise across technology, te reo Māori, tikanga, and Te Tiriti understanding.

"It needs to be someone with the technology, the te reo Māori knowledge, and the knowledge of Te Tiriti. And it's probably not always just one person."

— Karaitiana Taiuru, interview 2025

"The interesting thing about running your own business is that you can say no to things. I've had things come through, and we've decided, no, this is low-key disrespectful to our language, and we want no part of it. A corporation might not see that. But when you see everything — the whole project — you can."

— Research participant, interview 2025
04

Community Authority and Co-Design as the Only Viable Path

Community is not a stakeholder — it is the source of authority. AI development must begin with community, be shaped with community, and remain accountable throughout. Technology can be a pathway into deeper human connection — but it cannot substitute for it. The goal is always to bring people to the marae, to the kaumātua, to the language in its living form.

"An indigenous-led AI development would bring the spiritual aspect — because we are actually spiritual people, and our language is by default spiritual as well."

— Dan Te Whenua Walker, interview 2025

"The technology can help, but it will never replace that feeling of connection."

— Smith, interview 2025
05

The Persistent Gap Between Principle and Practice

The principles for ethical AI already exist. The challenge is translating them into practice. This is not a gap of intent — it is a gap of structure, resourcing, coordination, and practical guidance. The issue is not a lack of knowledge — it is the absence of architecture to act on what is already known.

"I can see that there was a demand. A lot of people talked about wanting to implement Māori data sovereignty, but they didn't know how. At the time, there wasn't much around how do you operationalise it — how do you put it into practice."

— Research participant, interview 2025

"There are a lot of silos. We're all out here trying to do something, and some people are working with others, but a lot of people don't want to work together — even though we're working toward the same goal of revitalising te reo Māori."

— Research participant, interview 2025

Some participants highlighted that organisations genuinely want to adopt Māori data sovereignty — they just don't know how. The gap isn't one of intent; it's one of practical, supported pathways.

— Research participant, interview 2025 (paraphrased)
Practical Guidance · From the Research

Before You Use AI

AI tools are already being used for te reo Māori — for translation, pronunciation, whakapapa research, and reconnection. Many people using them don't fully understand how they work, what happens to their data, or where the outputs are coming from. That's not a personal failing. It's a structural one. But it matters — because the risks aren't future risks. They're happening now.

These questions are for anyone using AI to engage with te reo Māori or Māori cultural knowledge — whether you're reconnecting with your language, working in an organisation, or just trying to get a translation right.

Question 1

Do you know what the tool is actually doing with your input?

When you type te reo Māori into an AI tool — or ask it to translate something — that input may be stored, used to improve the model, and processed in ways you haven't consented to. Many widely used AI tools were trained on data scraped from the internet, including Māori language content collected without community knowledge or approval.

Ask yourself: Does this tool have a clear data policy? Does it store my inputs? Was it trained with Māori community consent?

Question 2

Is the output actually correct — or just confident?

AI tools sound certain even when they're wrong. Research in Aotearoa has found that AI-generated te reo Māori can appear accurate while being culturally off — mistranslating concepts, assigning gender where none exists in the language, or stripping whakapapa and tikanga from terms that can't be separated from them.

The real danger for those reconnecting with their language: a mistranslation doesn't just mislead you — it can fill gaps left by colonisation with something that feels right but isn't grounded in anything real.

Ask yourself: Have I validated this output with a fluent speaker or a trusted Māori language resource? Am I treating AI as a starting point or a final answer?

Question 3

Is what you're sharing noa, tapu, or rāhui?

Not all Māori data is the same. Before sharing anything with an AI system, consider which category it falls into:

  • Noa — publicly available, can generally be used with care
  • Tapu — sensitive cultural content that requires proper authority and should not leave Aotearoa
  • Rāhui — sacred knowledge (whakapapa, medicines, knowledge held within specific families) that should never be digitised or shared with AI systems

If you're unsure which category something falls into, that uncertainty is itself a signal to pause before sharing.

Question 4

Are you using AI as a bridge — or letting it become the destination?

AI can be a useful pathway for people reconnecting with te reo Māori — it can make the language more accessible, provide a starting point, and reduce the barrier of not knowing where to begin. That's real, and it matters.

But the research is clear: AI cannot replace the warmth and context of a person, a class, a marae, or a kaumātua. If AI is helping you get to those things, that's the technology working well. If it's becoming a substitute for them, that's worth questioning.

"The technology can help, but it will never replace that feeling of connection."

— Smith, interview 2025
Question 5

Are you informed enough to push back if something feels wrong?

AI literacy is the foundation of meaningful engagement. You don't need to be a technologist — but you do need enough understanding to notice when something doesn't feel right, to question outputs, and to make decisions about what you share and what you don't.

Disengaging from AI entirely isn't a safe option — these systems will shape how te reo Māori is encountered and represented whether or not you're involved. But uninformed participation carries its own risks. The most important thing is to engage with your eyes open.

"We're at a crossroads. We can either say, no, AI is bad, let's ignore it, and then we will be recolonised, but in a way that no one can comprehend right now… the risk is if we don't actually grasp it now."

— Karaitiana Taiuru, interview 2025

Where to go instead of (or alongside) AI

If you're reconnecting with te reo Māori, these are places built with community authority and cultural grounding:

  • Te Aka Māori Dictionary — te-aka.maori.nz — the most trusted reference for te reo Māori
  • Kōrero Māori — korero.maori.nz — language learning with cultural context
  • Kahu Code — kahuco.de — Māori-owned AI translation built with community oversight
  • Te Taura Whiri i te Reo Māori — tetaurawhiri.govt.nz — the Māori Language Commission

These questions come directly from the research findings. They are not a checklist — they are an invitation to engage more consciously with tools that are already shaping how te reo Māori is experienced in the world.

Deep Dive

Data Sovereignty in Practice

A framework for classifying Māori data within AI contexts — moving from abstract principle into operational decision-making before any AI system is built or procured.

Noa Open / Unrestricted

Data already in the public domain. This can be digitised and used within AI systems. It carries no special restriction — freely accessible and appropriate for open engagement.

Noa
Tapu Controlled / Restricted

Sensitive cultural data that should be kept within Aotearoa. Requires careful custodianship and clear authority over who may access it and how. Not for open digitisation or global AI training.

Tapu
Rāhui Sacred / Must Not Be Digitised

Whakapapa, sacred knowledge, medicines, and knowledge held within specific families. This should never be digitised or fed into AI systems. Its protection is non-negotiable.

Rāhui

"We can't rely on other people's principles. We need to create our own, and we need tino rangatiratanga, mana motuhake to do that. And that resides back with our hapū and our marae."

— Karaitiana Taiuru, interview 2025

Interview Findings · Deep Dive

AI & Indigenous
Storytelling

Storytelling in te ao Māori is not merely communication — it is the transmission of identity, whakapapa, and mana across generations. Two questions in particular drew the most honest, personal, and complex responses across all interviews.

Question 1

What does success look like in 10 years?

Sovereignty and ownership were central to almost every response. Participants envisioned indigenous communities — not organisations or governments — owning and controlling their own AI systems, with data stored on community-held infrastructure. The idea of AI being locked down with full kaitiakitanga was raised repeatedly, echoing the model demonstrated by Te Hiku Media.

Legislative reform was highlighted as a prerequisite. Existing laws — the Copyright Act, Privacy Act, and harmful digital communications legislation — were described as already decades out of date, leaving mātauranga Māori exposed and unprotected.

Education and workforce pathways featured strongly. Participants imagined a world where indigenous people are leading in AI careers and where wānanga are offering AI training — potentially drawing people away from traditional universities. One participant felt this shift could begin within five years, not ten.

Sovereignty & Ownership

Each major iwi having their own AI capability — built by and for their community. Data on community-held servers. AI locked down with full kaitiakitanga, like Te Hiku Media's model.

Legislative Reform

Copyright Act, Privacy Act, and digital harm laws described as decades out of date. Success in 2035 means those gaps are closed and mātauranga Māori is protected in law.

Global Collaboration

Indigenous AI models shared and respected across borders — Māori, Native American, and other communities learning from each other. Though others cautioned that politics often gets in the way.

"Not one indigenous sovereign AI — we will have multiple, owned 100% by indigenous communities, not organisations."

— Lee Timutimu, interview 2025
Question 2

"How would you feel if AI told your story to future generations — knowing there could be biases, different versions from different whānau members?"

This prompted the most personal and emotionally honest responses across all interviews. Feelings were mixed, nuanced, and often contradictory — reflecting the genuine complexity of the issue.

Strong Desire for Control

Most participants felt AI should not tell their story without direct input. Stories are owned at the whānau level — control over one's narrative should stay with family, not be handed to a machine or organisation.

Openness — With Conditions

Some were comfortable in principle if they had contributed directly. One person drew a firm line at their private life, while feeling at peace with their documented professional life already online.

Scepticism About Authenticity

AI could archive stories — but cannot replace the warmth and connection of a person telling a story at a tangi, around a table, in the moment. Some preferred AI to produce a written record, not perform storytelling.

Fear of Bias & Distortion

Different whānau members hold different versions of the same person. The data fed in shapes what comes out — like Chinese whispers. The solution: present multiple perspectives rather than flattening them.

Multiple Truths as Valid

"That's quite a Western construct — we always talk about there being multiple truths. That's your truth. This is mine." AI's drive toward singular, statistically likely outputs conflicts with this worldview.

Generational Perspective

What feels uncomfortable now — like a Black Mirror episode — future generations may consider completely normal. The real question isn't how we feel about it today. It's what our mokopuna will think is ordinary.

"The stories are still there, still correct and still precious. I'm not feared of AI anymore."

— Francie Diver, interview 2025

From the Miro Board · Community Voice

Community Conditions
for Trust

Trust in AI is not automatic, and it is not unconditional. These six conditions emerged as the minimum requirements communities need before genuinely trusting AI with their language and stories.

Accuracy Must Be Near-Perfect

AI working with te reo Māori cannot afford to be approximately right. Cultural and linguistic errors carry real harm — misrepresenting meaning, whakapapa, or tikanga in ways that may not be immediately visible.

Māori Control

Communities must hold genuine decision-making authority — not advisory roles. Control over what data is used, how it is stored, who can access it, and when the system can be shut down or changed.

Transparency

Communities need to understand how AI systems work with their data — in plain language, not technical terms. Opacity is incompatible with trust, especially given histories of extraction and misrepresentation.

Safety from Harm

Systems must actively prevent misuse — including deepfakes using Māori faces or voices, fake Māori identities, and the weaponisation of cultural signals for manipulation or exploitation.

Intergenerational Protection

Whatever is built must consider not just present use, but what future generations will inherit. Stories, data, and language entered into AI systems today will shape what mokopuna encounter tomorrow.

Consent & Regular Review

Consent must be ongoing and contextual — not a one-time sign-off. Systems should be reviewed regularly (every 3–12 months) with communities retaining the right to withdraw consent at any point.

Research Grounding

Frameworks That
Guided This Work

This research is grounded in two interconnected frameworks — not as external impositions, but as living guides that shaped how the research was conducted, why it is necessary, and who it is for.

Te Ara Tika

Kaupapa Māori Ethical Framework

Te Ara Tika grounds this research in four core principles that guide not only how research is conducted, but why it is necessary — to uphold mana, protect taonga, and contribute to safe, culturally grounded practice.

  • Whakapapa — Building meaningful and genuine relationships, honouring connection to     people, place, and lineage
  • Tika — Upholding integrity across all aspects of the research and design
  • Manaakitanga — Creating space that values cultural and social background, where participants can share honestly
  • Mana — Ensuring both the research and participants' words are honoured and treated with the respect they deserve

Kaupapa Māori Research

Indigenous Research Methodology

Kaupapa Māori research centres Māori values, language, priorities, and worldview. It recognises the researcher's responsibility as a Māori researcher reconnecting with language and whakapapa.

  • Research that serves the community it engages with, not just the academy
  • Supports deeper exploration of how AI interacts with Māori language and knowledge systems
  • Elevates Māori voices and grounds findings in lived experience
  • Ensures research upholds relational accountability; community benefit is prioritised
  • A Māori-first, tikanga-led approach — because only Māori can determine appropriate use and protection of their language

Recommendations

Practical actions, not high-level aspirations. They come directly from what participants shared and connect back to the research findings.

For Organisations
For Government
For Māori Communities
Using Te Reo Māori
1

Apply noa/tapu/rāhui data classification before any AI development or procurement begins. The default must be: Māori data belongs to the community unless explicit, informed permission for a specific use has been given.

2

Bring Māori governance in at the design brief stage, not the review stage. Co-design from the beginning is the only approach consistently associated with outcomes that work and communities that trust the process.

3

Build governance structures with real authority, not advisory roles. Tikanga-based governance requires three elements held collectively: technical AI expertise, te reo Māori and tikanga knowledge, and grounded understanding of Te Tiriti.

4

Stop relying on cultural taxation. Expecting one Māori staff member to carry all cultural responsibility is not governance. This work must be properly resourced through dedicated roles and fairly compensated external expertise.

5

Make values alignment with te ao Māori explicit before engagement begins. Many organisations already hold compatible values — mapping these alongside manaakitanga and kaitiakitanga shifts engagement from performative to genuinely grounded.

1

Move beyond voluntary guidance to enforceable standards. The Algorithm Charter demonstrated the consequences of voluntarism at scale. Enforceable standards with audit requirements and independent Māori oversight are the only mechanism for consistent change.

2

Treat data sovereignty as a Te Tiriti obligation, not a policy preference. Te reo Māori is a taonga. New Zealand is also a UNDRIP signatory — rights affirmed under UNDRIP extend into the digital age and apply directly to AI governance.

3

Invest in Māori AI governance infrastructure as essential, not optional. Māori represent less than 1% of the AI workforce in Aotearoa. Sustained multi-year investment is the only pathway to closing this gap.

4

Address the double digital divide as a precondition for meaningful participation. Investment in digital equity and AI literacy for Māori communities is what makes everything else on this list possible.

5

Develop specific policy for generative AI and mātauranga Māori, developed in genuine partnership with Māori experts. Current AI strategies are too broad to address the specific risks to Māori language and cultural knowledge.

1

Use collective governance structures to own and govern AI systems, not just advise on them. AI systems built for Māori communities should sit within structures that already know how to hold authority and accountability.

2

Exercise the right to say no — and understand this is a legal right, not just a cultural preference. Free, prior, and informed consent includes the right to withhold consent.

3

Trust the knowledge holders as the ultimate governance authority. The most important resource is not a framework — it is the people who carry true stories, accurate whakapapa, and tikanga held through generations.

4

Build stronger connections across the Māori AI ecosystem. The diversity of iwi and hapū approaches is a strength — but most powerful when connected through shared learning and aligned frameworks.

1

Recognise that te reo Māori is not translation — it is a different way of seeing the world. It carries cultural meaning, context, and whakapapa that cannot be transferred word-for-word from English.

2

Do not rely on AI without human validation from fluent speakers or language experts. AI-generated te reo can appear correct while being culturally wrong. Validation must be built into the process from the start.

3

Be honest about the difference between use and respect. Using te reo for appearance without genuine understanding causes harm — to the language, to the communities whose taonga it is, and to the organisation's credibility.

4

Align organisational values with te ao Māori before beginning. Mapping existing values alongside manaakitanga, kaitiakitanga, and whanaungatanga shifts the question from 'how do we add Māori language?' to 'do we hold the values that make this use meaningful?'

Closing Reflection

Eyes Wide Open

This research confirms that the crossroads is real. The window for Māori agency is open — but it will not stay open forever. What is clear is that we already know what good looks like. Tikanga-based governance, community-led design, data sovereignty, and collective decision-making are not new ideas. They exist. They are already being practised.

I did not start this research with all the answers. I started it because something did not feel right. I could see language and data being treated as something to extract, process, and commercialise — without any real understanding of what sits behind it.

"Make it ours, not be led by an artificial God. Eyes wide open, always wide open." — Francie Diver, interview 2025

What is needed now is not more discussion about what good looks like. We already know. What is needed is action — structure, resourcing, governance, and accountability. A shift from intention to implementation.
